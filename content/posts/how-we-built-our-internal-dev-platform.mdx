Developer platforms fail when they centralize control but ignore developer ergonomics. We designed ours around paved roads, not ticket queues.

## Platform thesis

The platform owns golden paths for application bootstrap, secret delivery, observability, and environment provisioning. Product teams keep autonomy over product logic and release cadence.

## Service templates as leverage

We built opinionated templates for Next.js apps, APIs, and workers. Each template ships with CI, policy checks, logging, and deployment defaults.

```bash
pnpm create unroll-service --template web-app
pnpm unroll env pull staging
pnpm unroll deploy --preview
```

## Security baked into workflows

Security reviews became lighter once guardrails moved into the platform itself. Teams inherit SSO, secrets rotation, baseline rate limits, and event logging by default.

## Observability first, not last

Every service emits traces, metrics, and structured logs on day one. That kept incident response fast and made platform adoption self-reinforcing.

### Lessons learned

Avoid overfitting the platform to one team. Strong primitives and great documentation outperform hyper-custom abstractions.

<Callout type="info">
The best platform feature is often the one that removes a manual checklist item without introducing a new approval step.
</Callout>
